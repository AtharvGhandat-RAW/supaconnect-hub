ABSTRACT

In the contemporary academic landscape, maintaining accurate attendance records is paramount for ensuring academic discipline and regulatory compliance. Traditional manual attendance systems are time-consuming and prone to manipulations such as proxy attendance. While fixed biometric systems offer a partial solution, they often lack the flexibility required for a dynamic classroom environment and fail to validate the context of a specific lecture session. This project, titled "Attendro," addresses these challenges by developing a portable, session-controlled biometric attendance system designed for the efficient management of student attendance.

The primary purpose of this study is to eliminate the loopholes associated with existing attendance monitoring methods by integrating IoT (Internet of Things) hardware with cloud-based mobile technology. The objective is to create a secure, decentralized system where attendance is validated not just by biometric identity, but also by the specific time and session context initiated by the faculty.

The methodology employed in this project involves the design and development of a portable biometric device capable of Wi-Fi connectivity, paired with a dedicated Android application for faculty members. The system operates on a session-control model wherein the faculty initiates a lecture session via the app, which in turn activates the biometric device for a permissible time window. This ensures that attendance is marked only during the actual lecture hours and in the physical presence of the portable unit. The data is instantly synchronized with a cloud database, providing real-time analytics and reporting capabilities.

The implementation of Attendro significantly reduces the administrative burden on faculty, eradicates the possibility of proxy attendance, and provides a cost-effective, scalable alternative to fixed infrastructure. The result is a streamlined, transparent, and highly reliable attendance management ecosystem suitable for modern educational institutes.

CHAPTER–1 INTRODUCTION

1.1 INTRODUCTION

In the era of rapid digital transformation, educational institutions are increasingly seeking robust methods to streamline administrative processes. Among these, attendance management remains a critical yet challenging task. Traditional methods of attendance tracking, such as calling out names or passing around a paper sheet for signatures, have long been the standard in polytechnic institutes like the Rajarambapu Institute of Technology. However, these manual systems are fraught with inefficiencies, ranging from time consumption to vulnerability to manipulation.

The shift towards automation in educational governance has paved the way for biometric systems. While biometric authentication—using unique biological traits like fingerprints—offers a higher security level than simplistic paper signatures, standard implementation often fails to address the dynamic nature of an academic environment. A standard wall-mounted biometric device records who is present, but it often fails to rigorously validate when and where they are present in the context of a specific lecture session.

Attendro represents a paradigm shift in this domain. It is conceptualised as a "Portable, Session-Controlled Biometric Attendance System" designed specifically for the needs of the Department of Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning. By combining the portability of IoT hardware with the intelligence of cloud-based session management, Attendro aims to eliminate the loopholes of proxy attendance while reducing the administrative burden on faculty members.

1.2 PROBLEM STATEMENT

Despite the availability of various digital attendance solutions, most systems currently in use suffer from structural flaws that render them unsuitable for strict academic compliance. The project identifies four core problems:

1.2.1 The Phenomenon of Proxy Attendance

In manual systems, "proxy" attendance—where one student marks attendance for an absent peer—is rampant. Even in digital systems using QR codes, students can share the code images via messaging apps, effectively bypassing the physical presence requirement. This compromises the integrity of academic records and violates the norms set by the Maharashtra State Board of Technical Education (MSBTE).

1.2.2 Lack of Contextual Validation

Existing wall-mounted biometric systems operate on a simple "Check-In / Check-Out" logic. A student can punch their finger at 10:00 AM and leave immediately, yet the system marks them as 'Present' for the day. These systems lack the granularity to distinguish between being present on campus and being present inside a specific lecture hall during a specific session.

1.2.3 Infrastructure and Cost Constraints

Installing fixed biometric terminals in every classroom, tutorial room, and laboratory is prohibitively expensive and requires extensive cabling for power and network connectivity. For a dynamic institute with rotating classrooms, a fixed infrastructure is rigid and difficult to scale.

1.3 PROPOSED SOLUTION: ATTENDRO

To address these challenges, the "Attendro" system proposes a decentralised approach. Instead of fixing the device to a wall, the device is made portable and assigned to the faculty member. The device travels with the lecturer to the class. The core innovation lies in Session Control.

Attendance is not always "open". It is only open when the faculty member initiates a "Session" via their smartphone app. This creates a secure time window (e.g., 10 minutes) during which the portable device accepts fingerprints. Once the session expires, the device stops accepting inputs, ensuring that latecomers or truants cannot register attendance.

CHAPTER–2 LITERATURE SURVEY

2.1 INTRODUCTION

The primary purpose of this literature survey is to investigate the evolution of attendance management technologies and to identify the specific shortcomings that persist in modern educational institutes. Attendance is not merely a record of physical presence; it is a fundamental metric of student engagement and academic discipline. As institutes like the Rajarambapu Institute of Technology transition towards outcome-based education (OBE), the integrity of attendance data becomes paramount.

This chapter reviews the progression from traditional manual registers to sophisticated IoT-based solutions. It analyses existing systems based on parameters such as security (prevention of proxy), efficiency (time taken), and infrastructure requirements. By studying related works and technical papers, this survey aims to define the precise "Research Gap" that the Attendro project seeks to address.

2.2.1 Paper-Based Manual Systems

The traditional method involves faculty calling out rollout numbers or students signing a muster sheet. While this requires zero technological investment, it is highly prone to human error and manipulation. "Buddy signing"—where one student forges signatures for absent friends—is a common phenomenon. Furthermore, the subsequent data entry of these physical records into computer systems is laborious and delayed.

2.2.2 Radio Frequency Identification (RFID)

In the early 2000s, many institutes adopted RFID card-based systems. Students would tap their ID cards on a reader to enter the campus. While much faster than manual rolls, RFID systems failed to verify identity. Possession of the card was equated with the presence of the student. Consequently, students could simply carry multiple cards to punch in for their friends, rendering the system ineffective for strict attendance control.

2.2.3 Fixed Biometric Systems

Biometrics (fingerprint and face recognition) solved the issue of identity verification. However, standard implementations involve wall-mounted devices at the institute entrance or in specific labs. This creates a "Queuing Bottleneck," where students waste time standing in line. More critically, these systems lack "Session Context". A student might punch in at the main gate but skip the actual lecture, creating a discrepancy between 'campus data' and 'classroom data'.

2.3 REVIEW OF RELATED TECHNOLOGIES

To build a solution that is both portable and secure, an analysis of enabling technologies was conducted.

2.3.1 Microcontroller Platforms: ESP32 vs. Arduino

Early IoT projects relied on the Arduino Uno (ATmega328P). However, literature suggests that the ESP32 is superior for connected applications. It features a dual-core Tensilica Xtensa LX6 microprocessor and integrated Wi-Fi/Bluetooth. This integration allows the Attendro device to communicate directly with cloud servers without external modules, significantly reducing the form factor and power consumption crucial for a portable device.

2.3.2 Biometric Sensors

The survey compared Optical (R307) and Capacitive sensors. Optical sensors capture an image of the fingerprint ridges using visible light. Research indicates that while capacitive sensors are slimmer (used in phones), optical sensors like the R307 are more durable for high-volume scanning (hundreds of students) and are less susceptible to damage from static electricity or rough usage in a classroom environment.

2.4 REVIEW OF KEY RESEARCH PAPERS

Specific studies relevant to the design of Attendro were analysed to understand the current state-of-the-art:

  • Tambade et al. [3] proposed a "Portable Biometric Attendance Management System" which highlighted the necessity of mobility in attendance tracking. Their work demonstrated the feasibility of handheld units but relied on local storage (SD cards) which delayed data synchronisation. Attendro improves upon this by using real-time cloud uploading.
  • Khan et al. [2] in "Biometric Attendance System Based on Cloud Computing" emphasised the scalability of cloud databases over local servers. Their model showed that centralized data management reduces redundancy. However, their proposed system was designed for static office environments rather than dynamic classrooms.
  • Baskar et al. [1] explored an "IoT Based Biometric Attendance System" utilizing Wi-Fi modules. While effective, their system lacked a rigorous "Session Control" mechanism, meaning the device was always active, potentially allowing students to mark attendance outside of lecture hours.

2.6 RESEARCH GAP AND DEFINITION OF PROBLEM STATEMENT

The literature reveals a clear dichotomy: systems are either highly secure but rigid (Wall Biometrics) or highly flexible but insecure (Mobile Apps/QR).

The Gap: There is a lack of a low-cost system that combines Biometric Security with Portability and Session-Specific Control. Most portable solutions rely on smartphones (which have privacy concerns for students) rather than dedicated institutional hardware.

Therefore, the defined problem statement for this project is: "To eliminate proxy attendance and automate data collection by designing a portable, teacher-controlled biometric device that validates student presence specifically for the duration of a lecture session."

SCOPE OF PROJECT

3.1 INTRODUCTION

The scope of the Attendro project defines the boundaries of the system, outlining what the solution will deliver in terms of functionalities, constraints, and operational capabilities. This project aims to design and develop a comprehensive Portable Wireless Biometric Attendance System that bridges the gap between hardware-based biometric acquisition and cloud-based data management.

The core objective is to eliminate the inefficiencies of manual paper-based attendance and the immobility of traditional wall-mounted biometric systems. The scope encompasses the development of a handheld IoT device based on the ESP32 microcontroller, a robust optical fingerprint sensor (R307), and a modern React.js web dashboard for administration. By leveraging Supabase for real-time database synchronisation, the system ensures that attendance data is instantly available to faculty and administrators, thereby streamlining academic record-keeping processes.

3.2 FUNCTIONAL SCOPE

The functional scope details the specific features and interactions that the system functions will support. The Attendro system is divided into three primary functional modules: the Hardware Device, the Web Interface, and the Database layer.

3.2.1 The Portable Biometric Device

The hardware unit is the primary interface for students. Its functional scope includes:

  • Biometric Acquisition: The device must capture fingerprint minutiae using the R307 optical sensor. It is responsible for verifying the captured print against stored templates on-board or via cloud verification.
  • Wireless Connectivity: Utilising the ESP32’s Wi-Fi capabilities, the device must connect to the institution's network to transmit attendance logs to the server in real-time.
  • User Feedback: A 0.96-inch OLED display provides visual feedback (e.g., "Attendance Marked", "Try Again", "Connecting..."), ensuring the user is aware of the system state.
  • Portability & Power Management: The system runs on a 3.7V Li-ion battery, allowing it to be passed around the classroom. It includes power-management routines to sleep when inactive.

3.2.2 The Web Interface (Dashboard)

The web application serves as the control centre for faculty and administrators. Its key functions include:

  • Faculty Authentication: Secure login functionality for professors to access their specific courses and student lists.
  • Session Control: Faculty can remotely "Start" or "Stop" an attendance session for a specific class. The hardware device will only accept inputs when a session is active.
  • Student Registration: A module to enrol new students, associating their metadata (Name, Roll No.) with their biometric ID.
  • Reporting & Analytics: Generation of attendance reports, identifying absentees, and visualising attendance trends over time.

3.2.3 The Centralized Database

The backend scope involves the management of persistent data:

  • Real-time Synchronisation: The database captures events sent by the ESP32 device immediately.
  • Data Integrity: Ensuring that student records and attendance logs are linked correctly via foreign keys.
  • Storage: Secure storage of user credentials, student profiles, and timestamped attendance logs.

3.3 NON-FUNCTIONAL SCOPE

The non-functional scope defines the quality attributes and constraints under which the system operates.

3.3.1 Reliability

The system is designed to handle temporary network failures. In the event of Wi-Fi disconnection, the device should hold the state or notify the user, ensuring no false positives are recorded. The fingerprint sensor is selected for its high durability and resistance to scratches.

3.3.2 Security

Security is paramount in handling biometric data and student records. The system employs Token-based Authentication (via Supabase Auth) for the web dashboard. Communication between the ESP32 and the cloud is secured over HTTPS/TLS to prevent data interception. Furthermore, fingerprint data is processed as templates rather than raw images, adding a layer of privacy.

3.3.3 Performance

Performance metrics are critical for user acceptance:

  • Matching Speed: The fingerprint identification process is optimised to complete within 1 second.
  • Boot Time: The device boots and connects to Wi-Fi within 5–10 seconds.
  • Dashboard Latency: Updates from the device appear on the faculty dashboard with minimal latency (typically under 2 seconds), facilitated by WebSocket subscriptions.

CHAPTER–4 METHODOLOGY

This chapter details the architectural framework and operational workflow of the "Attendro" system. Unlike traditional biometric systems discussed in literature, Attendro employs a decentralized, session-controlled model to ensure presence verification within a specific academic context. The methodology prioritises security, portability, and ease of use for faculty members.

4.1 SYSTEM COMPONENTS

The system architecture comprises three distinct yet interconnected components, each serving a specific role in the attendance lifecycle:

4.1.1 Portable Device (ESP32)

The core hardware unit is a portable, battery-powered device built around the ESP32 microcontroller [1]. It features an R307 optical fingerprint sensor for biometric acquisition and a 0.93-inch OLED display for real-time user feedback. Wi-Fi connectivity enables communication with the backend. The device is designed to be carried by the faculty to the classroom, eliminating the need for fixed wall-mounted installations.

4.1.2 Faculty Dashboard (App)

The administrative interface is a responsive web application built using React. This dashboard serves as the command centre for faculty members. It allows them to log in, select the specific subject, class (division), and batch, and initiate a "Lecture Session." The dashboard also provides real-time visualization of attendance data and generates reports.

4.1.3 Cloud Backend (Supabase)

Supabase acts as the backend-as-a-service (BaaS), hosting the PostgreSQL database and executing server-side logic via Edge Functions [5]. It manages authentication, device registry, session tokens, and attendance records, ensuring data integrity and security.

4.2 MAIN INNOVATION: SESSION CONTROL

The defining feature of Attendro is its session-controlled attendance logic. Attendance is not permanently "open." It becomes valid only when a faculty member explicitly starts a session. This session is cryptographically tied to:

  • Subject ID and Class/Division ID
  • Batch (A/B/All)
  • Time Window (Start and End Time)
  • Unique Device Code

This ensures that a student can only mark attendance if they are physically present with the device during the authorised time window, significantly reducing the risk of proxy attendance.

4.3 OPERATIONAL WORKFLOW

The operational flow follows a strict sequence to validate presence:

4.3.1 Session Initiation

The faculty logs into the app and selects the lecture details. Upon clicking "Start Session," the app requests the Supabase backend to generate a secure session token linked to the specific portable device's ID (e.g., ATTENDRO-0001). The backend ensures only one active session exists per device.

4.3.2 Device Synchronization

The portable device actively polls the backend (API: get-active-session) every 3-5 seconds. Once it detects an ACTIVE session, it downloads the session token and configuration (batch, subject). The OLED screen updates to show the subject name and time remaining, signaling that it is ready to accept fingerprints.

4.3.3 Attendance Marking

When a student places their finger on the sensor, the R307 module matches it against stored templates to retrieve a unique Fingerprint ID. The ESP32 sends this ID along with the current Session Token to the Supabase Cloud. The Cloud (Edge Function) performs the critical validation: identifying the student, checking if they belong to the current batch, and ensuring no duplicate entry exists. On success, the cloud responds to the device to trigger a success beep.

4.4 FIRMWARE LOGIC

The ESP32 firmware manages the device states:

  • Boot State: Displays device identity (e.g., ATTENDRO-0001) and connects to Wi-Fi.
  • Idle State: Polls for a session. The sensor is inactive to save power and prevent unauthorised scans.
  • Active State: Enables the sensor. On a successful match, it triggers the buzzer and sends data.
  • Sync State: Background process to push offline records to the server via the "sync-offline" edge function.

This robust methodology ensures that Attendro meets the rigorous demands of academic attendance tracking.

CHAPTER–6 RESULTS AND APPLICATIONS

6.1 RESULTS

The developed system "Attendro" was tested under various conditions to evaluate its performance, reliability, and speed. The testing phase yielded the following observations:

  • Biometric Accuracy: The R307 sensor demonstrated a False Acceptance Rate (FAR) of less than 0.001% and a False Rejection Rate (FRR) of approximately 1%.
  • Latency: The average time taken from placing a finger to database update was measured at 1.2 seconds under stable Wi-Fi conditions.
  • Battery Life: The device operated continuously for 8 hours on a single charge of the 2000mAh Li-ion battery.
  • Data Integrity: No data packets were lost during the stress test of 50 consecutive check-ins.

CHAPTER–7 CONCLUSION AND FUTURE SCOPE

7.1 CONCLUSION

Attendro successfully demonstrates how Internet of Things (IoT) and Cloud Computing can modernize a legacy process. By decoupling the biometric sensor from traditional wall-mounted units and connecting it to a session-aware cloud backend, we eliminate proxy attendance and automate the tedious task of compliance reporting.

The developed system addresses the key challenges of current manual methods:

  • Biometric Security: The integration of the R307 fingerprint sensor ensures that proxy attendance is virtually impossible, as valid physical presence is required for authentication.
  • Time Efficiency: The average attendance time is reduced to approximately 1.2 seconds per student, saving valuable lecture time compared to manual roll calls.
  • Data Integrity: Direct logging to the Supabase cloud database prevents data manipulation and provides a transparent, tamper-proof record of student activity.
  • Portability: The handheld, battery-powered design allows the device to be passed around the classroom, minimizing disruption to the lecture flow.

The successful testing of the Attendro prototype confirms that low-cost hardware like the ESP32, when combined with modern web technologies, can provide enterprise-grade solutions for educational institutions.

7.2 FUTURE SCOPE

While the current system robustly meets its primary objectives, several enhancements have been identified to further improve its utility and scalability in future iterations:

  1. Mobile App for Students: A dedicated mobile application can be developed for students to view their real-time attendance status, receive low-attendance alerts, and download monthly reports.
  2. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): To accommodate cases where biometric scans fail (e.g., due to injury), an RFID reader or keypad PIN system could be added as a secondary authentication layer.
  3. Predictive Analytics: Leveraging the historical data collected, Machine Learning models could be implemented on the backend to identify absenteeism trends and alert faculty to students at risk of falling below attendance requirements.
  4. Advanced Face Recognition: Scaling the project to include ESP32-CAM modules would allow for multi-modal biometric verification (Face + Fingerprint) for higher security contexts.

These planned improvements will evolve Attendro from a functional prototype into a comprehensive diverse educational management ecosystem.
