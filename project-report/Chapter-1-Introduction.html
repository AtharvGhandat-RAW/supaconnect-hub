Introduction

1.1 Introduction

In the dynamic landscape of educational administration, the accurate recording of student attendance remains a fundamental yet challenging task. Traditional methods of attendance tracking—primarily paper-based roll calls or sign-in sheets—have long been the standard in polytechnic institutes like the Rajarambapu Institute of Technology. However, these manual processes are increasingly proving to be inefficient, time-consuming, and prone to manipulation. As academic institutions strive for higher standards of accountability and data integrity, the limitations of archaic systems become glaringly obvious.

The advent of biometric technology promised a robust solution to identity verification. Early adopters integrated fingerprint scanners and facial recognition units into fixed entry points of buildings or classrooms. While this represented a significant leap forward in security, it introduced new rigidities. Fixed infrastructure requires substantial capital investment for installation in every lecture hall and laboratory. Moreover, a static device at a door cannot verify whether a student remains in the class for the duration of the lecture or merely "punches in" and leaves—a phenomenon that undermines the very purpose of attendance tracking.

This project, titled "Attendro: A Portable, Session-Controlled Biometric Attendance System with Centralized Fingerprint," proposes a paradigm shift. Moving away from fixed, location-centric systems, Attendro introduces a student-centric and session-centric approach. By coupling portable hardware with modern cloud synchronisation, the system empowers faculty members of the Department of Artificial Intelligence & Machine Learning to take attendance effortlessly, ensuring that the record reflects true classroom participation rather than simple presence on campus.

1.2 Problem Statement

The motivation for developing Attendro stems from observing the operational inefficiencies in the current attendance management workflows within technical institutes. Specifically, four critical problems have been identified:

1.2.1 Proxy Attendance Verification

In large classrooms, proxy attendance remains rampant. With manual roll calls, students often answer for absent friends. Even with basic digital solutions like QR codes, students can share the code images via messaging apps, allowing peers outside the classroom to mark their attendance remotely. This lack of verifiable physical presence compromises the integrity of academic records.

1.2.2 Lack of Session Context

Most biometric systems are designed for access control rather than academic tracking. A wall-mounted device records that a student entered a room at 10:00 AM, but it fails to link that entry to a specific academic session (e.g., "Data Structures Lecture"). Consequently, administrators cannot easily distinguish between a student who attended a class and one who merely used the room for self-study. There is a disconnect between the identity verification and the academic context.

1.2.3 Data Fragmentation and Latency

Currently, attendance data is often siloed in physical registers or isolated excel sheets maintained by individual faculty members. Collating this data to generate "Defaulter Lists" or monthly reports is a labour-intensive manual process that takes days. This latency prevents timely intervention for students who are falling behind.

1.2.4 Infrastructure Rigidity and Cost

Installing biometric terminals in every classroom, tutorial room, and laboratory is cost-prohibitive. Wiring these devices for power and ethernet connectivity adds to the complexity. A fixed infrastructure also lacks flexibility; if a lecture is rescheduled to a seminar hall or outdoors, the attendance system cannot follow the class.

1.3 Proposed Solution: Attendro

"Attendro" addresses these challenges by decentralising the hardware while centralising the data. The core of the solution is a Portable Biometric Device powered by an ESP32 microcontroller, which is carried by the faculty member to the class, much like a marker or a duster.

The workflow is designed for strictly "Session-Controlled" attendance:

  1. Session Initiation: The faculty member logs into the Attendro Mobile App/Web Portal and creates a session (e.g., "Subject: NLP, Batch: A, Time: 10:00-11:00").
  2. Device Activation: The system generates a unique Session Token. The portable device fetches this token via Wi-Fi and enters "Scanning Mode".
  3. Verification: Students press their finger on the sensor. The device locally matches the fingerprint against a cached session list or sends the template to the server for verification.
  4. Real-Time Logging: Successful matches are immediately logged to the cloud database (Supabase) against the specific session ID.
  5. Closure: Once the teacher ends the session, the device stops accepting inputs, preventing late attendance.

This approach ensures that attendance is only possible when the authorized faculty member is present and the session is active, effectively eliminating proxy attendance and ensuring context.

1.4 Objectives

The primary goal of this project is to develop a fully functional prototype of the Attendro system. The specific technical and functional objectives are:

  • To Develop Portable Hardware: To design a battery-operated, handheld unit using the ESP32 microcontroller and an optical fingerprint sensor (R307/similar) that can operate for a full academic day on a single charge.
  • To Implement Session Control Logic: To create a software architecture where biometric inputs are only valid within a time-bound window defined by the faculty, linking every scan to a specific academic subject and topic.
  • To Ensure Offline Resilience: To engineer the device to store attendance logs locally in non-volatile memory if Wi-Fi connectivity is lost, and automatically synchronise with the central server once connectivity is restored.
  • To Create a Centralised Management Dashboard: To develop a web-based interface for the HOD and faculty to view real-time statistics, manage student enrolment, and generate automated compliance reports (e.g., PDF/Excel exports).

1.5 Scope of the Project

In Scope:

  • Development of the ESP32 firmware for fingerprint scanning and Wi-Fi communication.
  • Creation of the backend database using Supabase (PostgreSQL) for user management and log storage.
  • Development of a Faculty Web Interface for starting sessions and viewing reports.
  • Implementation of "Store and Forward" logic for offline scenarios.
  • Biometric template management (enrolment and verification).

Out of Scope:

  • Integration with the institute's existing legacy ERP system (standalone operation is prioritised).
  • Facial recognition or Iris scanning biometric modalities.
  • Hardware casing manufacturing (3D printing is conceptual, not industrial grade).

1.6 Significance of the Project

For the Department of AI & ML at Rajarambapu Institute of Technology, the deployment of Attendro signifies a step towards a "Smart Campus." It reduces the administrative burden on faculty, allowing them to focus more on teaching rather than record-keeping. The transparency of the digital records builds trust between students and administration. Furthermore, the generated data can provide insights into student behaviour patterns, correlating attendance with academic performance, which is invaluable for the educational ecosystem.
